# The Best Productivity System for Students

## And some optional tips to get you started

> We often assume that productivity means getting more things done each day. Wrong. Productivity is getting important things done consistently.
> -James Clear

This quote was game-changing for me. Most of the time when you hear the word productivity, you associate it with getting a lot of things done in a limited amount of time.  
What good does that do you if you get nothing done well?

To be productive, you need to know what you need to get done, give yourself time to do it, and then do it with as much focus as possible.

As Cal Newport says,

> Work Accomplished = Time Spent x Intensity

I’ve found that it’s nearly impossible to be “productive” by this definition without some sort of productivity system.

So what is a productivity system? A productivity system is simply any system that helps you manage everything you need to get done. You probably have one in place right now without much thinking about it.

Maybe you tell a friend to remind you about major exams, or you email yourself to do assignments. If that’s working for you, more power to you, but I find that I’m far more efficient at getting my work done when I’m intentional about how I systemize everything needed to be a productive student. And chances are, you will be too.

I used to keep my endless to-dos and calendar in my head and trusted that to get everything done. Unsurprisingly, I kept forgetting tasks. What’s worse is that I had a baseline level of stress from trying to remember them. This meant that I wasn’t fully present even when I did remember what to do.

In this post, I’m going to list the four general features I’d recommend for a simple student productivity system, the apps I use to implement them (but analog works perfectly fine too), and some tips for those apps based on my experience.

## Here are the “Four Managements” that make up my ultimate student productivity system

### Time Management (a calendar)

This is pretty self-explanatory, but I still find it crazy that I didn’t use a calendar before embarking on my productivity journey. Once you start using one, it’s hard to imagine living any other way.

A complaint you might have is that you dislike having constraints. If so, you’re like me, but I would still recommend scheduling a very bare-bones outline of what you intend your day to look like. Trust me, you will feel freer without the cognitive burden of having tons of options to choose from and be more likely to use your time intentionally.

Here’s another tip — managing energy and not time. This is the new big idea in the productivity sphere, and for good reason.

Perform energy-demanding tasks at times when you have high levels of energy and do activities that raise your energy levels when they’re low.

By managing your energy levels in this way, you increase the odds that you are focusing while you work while having time to decompress.

Experiment with yourself, and try to answer the questions:
1. When are you most creative?
2. When are you most focused?
3. Do you do better getting the hardest thing in the day done first and building momentum downwards, or do you prefer getting smaller tasks out of the way to build momentum into a major task?
4. How does each class and type of assignment generally affect your energy levels?

This might be highly controversial, but I would avoid things like social media as much as possible when you want to recharge your energy.

Social media is the easiest thing to start, so that’s what we default to when we have low energy levels. However, it’s usually not particularly energy-boosting and can often sink way more time than you intended.

### Task Management (a to-do list)

You can track most of your “big rocks” in your calendar, but it becomes a hassle to fit all of your “little rocks” into your calendar.

My solution to this is a to-do list.

I use my calendar as my broad outline for what I want my day to look like, and my to-do list to keep track of all the little side tasks that I have to get done.

You want your to-do list to be as frictionless as possible so you can quickly capture tasks and ideas as they pop into your head and then worry about getting them done later.

I’ve only realized how bad my brain is at remembering stuff when I started seeing forgotten major tasks just a few days after I’d scheduled them.

I used to have a reputation as a forgetter, but now people ask me to remember tasks for them because I’ve made it a habit to put any task — no matter how small — into my to-do list.

### Project Management

It’s really helpful to track all of the “projects” you have to do both inside and outside of school. Projects include assignments, essays, major exams, applications, and more.

Having something separate from your to-do list and calendar helps with visualizing long-term goals and tracking progress across stages. This should feed into your to-do list and calendar so you consistently work on larger goals.

### Personal Knowledge Management

While not strictly necessary, it’s useful to build the habit of interacting with the information you consume and preserving notes that resonate with you.

If you’re doing something creative like blogging or YouTube, this helps generate ideas and saves time when brainstorming.

Even if you’re not, saving notes on topics like personal finance or entrepreneurship can be helpful long term.

## Apps that make up the system

### Calendar: Google Calendar / Apple Calendar
The specific calendar doesn’t matter much. The key is having a system that is flexible enough to adjust to life’s changes.

### Task Management: Todoist
Todoist is recommended for its simplicity, organization features, and keyboard shortcuts. The advice is to use it on mobile and make it easily accessible so tasks can be added quickly.

### Project Management: Notion
Notion can be used for organizing notes, studying, projects, reading lists, and more. However, it’s easy to overcomplicate your system due to its flexibility, so it’s better to start simple and avoid over-optimizing.

### Knowledge Management: Evernote (optional)
Evernote is recommended for easier search and organization of notes compared to large Notion setups, especially when note volume becomes large.

## Conclusion

If you want to get these “4 managements” done in one app, watch videos by Thomas Frank where he demonstrates implementing time, task, and knowledge management in Notion.

For deeper productivity systems, the author recommends creators like Ali Abdaal and Cajun Koi Academy.

The key idea is that the productivity system is not the goal — the output it enables is what matters.

Set up the simplest possible system and use it until it breaks.
